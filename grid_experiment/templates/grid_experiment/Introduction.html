Instructions

Structure of the experiment

This experiment consists of three parts:

  1. Instructions, practice, and questions of understanding — You will read the instructions, complete a practice round, and answer a few questions to check your understanding.
  2. Main Task rounds — You will complete 28 rounds of the task.
  3. Questionnaire — At the end, you will answer a short questionnaire.

Payment: One round from the Main Task will be chosen at random at the end of the experiment. Your total earnings from that round will be paid out.

The two scenarios and your choices

In this experiment, you will make decisions that affect the payments of yourself and a charity.

In the Main Task, each round takes place in one of two scenarios. In every round, you will choose between Action A and Action B, and these choices determine how much you and the charity receive.

The charity: GiveDirectly

In this experiment, the charity that can receive money from your choices is GiveDirectly.

What is GiveDirectly?

This is how they describe themselves:

GiveDirectly is a nonprofit that lets donors like you send money directly to the world’s poorest households. We believe people living in poverty deserve the dignity to choose for themselves how best to improve their lives — cash enables that choice.

Research finds people use cash in impactful and creative ways. We read the research. We read more research. Then we did our research. And it turns out, that people use cash on medicine; cows and goats and chickens; school fees; water; solar lights; tin roofs; irrigation; motorcycles to jumpstart taxi services; businesses to generate income; and more.

If you think about it, doesn’t giving directly make sense? Cash allows individuals to invest in what they need, instead of relying on aid organizations and donors thousands of miles away to choose for them. Isn’t this what you would prefer?

Questions to check your understanding

Based on the tables above, please answer the following questions. Your answers here do not affect your earnings; they are just to help you understand the task.

1. In Scenario O, if you choose Action B, how much does the charity receive?

2. In Scenario I, if you want to give the charity more (compared to the other action), which action should you choose?

3. In Scenario O, if you want to give yourself more (compared to the other action), which action should you choose?

4. In Scenario I, if you choose the action that gives you more, what happens to the charity's earnings compared to the other action?

5. In Scenario O, which action is better for the charity (gives the charity more)?

Identifying which scenario is active

Before you choose between Actions A and B, you will see a grid filled with O and I symbols for 2 seconds. The symbol that appears more often in the grid determines which scenario (O or I) is active in that round.

You will then report which symbol you think appeared more frequently. If you are correct, you earn an identification bonus of {{ identification_bonus }}.

6. Suppose the true active scenario is Scenario O, but you mistakenly report Scenario I. Which scenario is used to calculate your and the charity’s earnings?

7. If you correctly identify the majority symbol in a round, what do you earn in addition to your action payoff?

Practice rounds and Main Task

First, you will play 4 practice rounds. These rounds include the feedback screen after each round so that you can become familiar with the task, the scenarios, and the round summary.

After the practice, you will play 28 Main Task rounds (for payment). In these rounds, you will not see the round summary after every single round, but your earnings are always calculated in the same way as described above.
